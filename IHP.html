The Problem

Healthcare organizations rarely fail because they lack talent.

Clinical-operational misalignment

Clinical and operational leadership pulling in different directions, slowing every decision and draining physician satisfaction.

Staff turnover

Turnover running 16 to 20% annually — with recruitment, onboarding, and lost productivity costs that compound quietly.

Revenue cycle dysfunction

Scheduling inefficiency, clinician underutilization, and revenue cycle breakdowns eroding income that never appears on a single line item.

Accreditation and compliance risk

Regulatory exposure and accreditation vulnerability that threaten the organization's ability to operate.

Cultural dysfunction

A demoralized culture producing defensive practice, higher incident rates, and patient experience failures.

Operational knowledge gaps

Critical knowledge living in people's heads rather than systems — making every departure a continuity crisis.

The average hospital loses $60,090 per departing bedside RN. Revenue cycle dysfunction costs a typical $50M hospital $7.5M or more annually. US hospital labor costs increased $42.5 billion from 2021 to 2023. These losses are recoverable.

Philosophy

You cannot fix what you have not accurately named.

Most organizations in decline aren't failing because they lack strategy. They're failing because the hidden incentives driving behavior aren't aligned with the organization's vision and mission.

The first thing we do when we walk into a failing organization is identify the real incentive structures and the informal power networks — the things that are not spoken aloud. We perform a 30-day diagnostic, name the problems, and build systems that realign incentives.

While doing systems-level work, we invest in individuals and develop leaders. Culture is an emergent phenomenon. The culture that emerges is shaped by systems and incentives. Change those, and the culture that emerges changes too.

Most organizations in crisis are already following their incentives — just not the ones written in the strategic plan. Identifying the actual incentive structures driving behavior, formal and informal, is where the real diagnostic work begins.
